    Weapon of the Week

Greatswords


A greatsword is a sword only in name.As the name suggests, a greatsword is held with two hands, buts its wielder must still be inhumanly strong. It is this great weight that sends foes flying when hit solidly.

The large motion that puts the weight of the body into the attack reflects the great size of their adversaries long ago.

Axes

Axes are powerful due to their weight, but one wrong swing leaves the wielder wide open, so timing and proximity to the enemy must be judged carefully.

Through the course of human history, commonplace objects have been pressed into service as weapons. Axes, by virtue of their ubiquity, are no exception. Besides axes designed for combat, there were many battle axes that doubled as tools.Battle axes generally weigh far less than modern splitting axes, especially mauls, because they were designed to cut legs and arms rather than wood; consequently, slightly narrow slicing blades are the norm. This facilitates deep, devastating wounds.

Polearms

Medieval polearms are what they lead you to believe they are: weapons on a pole. This pole could be anywhere from 5 to 16 feet long. And the term "polearm" covers a lot of different weapons, and these weapons changed over the centuries. The predominant advantage to a polearm was its reach. With it you could attack your enemy from a distance.
Versatile and effective, the class of weapons were hand-crafted to each serve a purpose on the battlefield. The halberd, the most popular of polearms was a long-hilted weapon mixing spear and axe is difficult to handle, requiring both strength and dexterity. The Halberd has two elementary attacks: spear-like thrusting and large sweeping swings. However, one false swing and the wielder is left wide open.
